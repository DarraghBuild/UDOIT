Beatriz Reyes-Foster

Course Introduction

Not that long ago, when I was a college sophomore at Rutgers University in New Jersey, my major advisor told me that in order to major in anthropology I had to take a course in “ling anth”, or linguistic anthropology. I must admit that at first, I felt really overwhelmed. The little bit that I had heard about what linguistic anthropologists studied seemed really difficult at best and kind of boring at worst. Terms I had heard thrown around by people familiar with the topic, terms such as morphology, syntax, phoneme, copula deletion, and ergative marker seemed really complicated and impenetrable. I was a cultural anthropology major –I wanted to go the field, hang out with some people, and learn about their culture. Language just seemed to be a means to an end: I had to learn the language of the people I was going to work with if I was going to communicate with them. But other than that, I didn’t really think there was much more else to language and its role in culture.

I LOVED my first ling anth class! My professor, Dr. Laura Ahearn, taught the course in a really accessible way and repeatedly demonstrated the ways in which language is much more than just a tool for communication: it does all kinds of cool things!

What kind of “cool things”?

Think about this example: Two Yucatec Maya speakers, Mercedes and Efraín, are having a conversation.

Mercedes:
Bix a beel, jEfraín?
Efraín:
Jach ma’alob, jMech, Toj in wool bejlae.

Mercedes just asked Efraín how he is doing, and Efraín responded that he is doing very well. However, if we were to translate this exchange word for word, this is what it would look like:

Mercedes:
How is your road, [male marker] Efraín?
Efraín:
Very good, [male marker]Mech, my heart is straight today.

What does this exchange tell us about how Maya people think about the world? The Yucatec Maya language is full of “path” imagery. When we part our hair, the white line of scalp that is visible is called a “path”. In an agricultural society that relies heavily on a form of agriculture called milpa, Maya people often have to travel long distances in the forest to get to their fields. Finding the right path is crucial to a family’s survival. Very often, people talk about life as a journey down a hopefully straight or “toj” path. Examining the semantics (meaning) and pragmatics (practice) of a language can thus really tell us something about the culture and people that speak it.

So what are we doing this semester?

As I said before, my interest in linguistic anthropology developed because I had a GREAT ling anth instructor. You may or may not have noticed, but the textbook I use in this class was written by Dr. Laura Ahearn. I love this book because she does in the book exactly what she did in the class: she explains complicated concepts in an accessible and engaging way. As your professor, I hope to do what she did: to show you how interesting and relevant understanding the connection between language and culture really is.

I hope we have a great semester together. I am excited to be teaching this class and I hope that I will do it effectively enough to make you excited about being a student in my class. I try to be approachable, challenging, and fair in my teaching. Please feel free to come to me, in person or electronically, at any time.
