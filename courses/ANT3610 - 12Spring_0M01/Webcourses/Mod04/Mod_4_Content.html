The Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis

The Axiomatic View of the Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis

Many researchers outside of anthropology have mistakenly used the term “Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis” to mean the simplistic notion that language determines thought. The idea that the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis runs in one direction, from language to thought, without any consideration for culture is simply incorrect. It is important to note that the term “Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis” itself is a misnomer because:

  • Sapir and Whorf never co-authored a text together.
  • The “hypothesis” does not satisfy the scientific requirements of a hypothesis in that is not falsifiable nor testable. In fact, Boas, Sapir, and Whorf never framed their research on language using the language of science.

Today, linguistic anthropologists prefer to take an axiomatic view of the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis, where language, thought, and culture are all mutually constitutive. An axiom is an assumption that informs all research, in other words, a statement or proposition that is accepted and taken for granted.

Theory of Mind and Language in General

False-Belief Tests

As you read in your textbook, linguists, psychologists, and linguistic anthropologists often look to children when they want to learn something about language. This is because young children are in the process of learning language, so observing how young children acquire language –how their abilities and perceptions about the world shift as language becomes an increasingly important filter through which they see the world—can tell us a lot about the nature of language itself and the relationship between language and thought.

A key concept in developmental psychology is theory of mind, our ability to read intent, emotions, and actions in others. It is central to our ability to empathize with others and to the development of our social skills. A false-belief test is a test devised to determine whether or not a child has developed theory of mind. This video shows a great example of a False-Belief test.
